Brand & Style Guide

The visual and verbal identity for CSD's website relaunch — guided by the Navigator of Possibility framework.

Navigator of Possibility

The two conceptual pillars that define the CSD brand. They work in tension and harmony.

Pillar 1

Possibility

We believe in every child's potential. We create brightness and belief, celebrate successes, and believe dreams are innately individualized and aspirational.

Design role: The dominant emotional register. Warmth, optimism, forward movement, light.

Pillar 2

Navigator

We guide families. We are trusted, reliable, and expert. We reduce overwhelm and cultivate hope and confidence.

Design role: The undercurrent that makes Possibility credible. Structure, hierarchy, clear pathways.

Weighting: Possibility leads — it should be the dominant feeling a family carries away. Navigator is the undercurrent that makes Possibility credible. Think of it as warmth supported by structure, not structure decorated with warmth. A site that feels genuinely aspirational and human, with clear evidence of expertise woven through, gets it right.

Audience & Emotional Context

Who we're designing for, and the emotional state they arrive in.

Primary Audience

Parents and caregivers of children with developmental, communication, or learning differences — often arriving at CSD feeling uncertain, overwhelmed, or having been bounced between providers without a clear path forward.

Gut-check for all design decisions:

Would an 11-year-old boy be embarrassed to be associated with this? If yes, pull back. The aesthetic should feel confident, forward-looking, and slightly aspirational.

Emotional Journey

When They Arrive

  • Anxious about what their child's diagnosis means
  • Exhausted from navigating fragmented systems
  • Looking for someone who "gets it"

When They Leave the Site

  • Relieved — there's a clear next step
  • Seen — this place understands kids like mine
  • Optimistic — things can be different from here
  • Confident — these people know what they're doing

Brand Personality

Think of the brand voice as a highly competent, warm friend who happens to be an expert. Not clinical. Not corporate. Not overly cheerful.

Accessibility & Contrast

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance is mandatory for all text on the CSD site. This means a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (18px+ bold or 24px+ regular). Non-text UI elements (icons, borders, form controls) require 3:1 against their background.

Target AAA (7:1) where possible for body copy to support users with low vision.

Rules

Do

  • Use Navy or Text Dark for body copy on white
  • Use White text on Navy backgrounds (15.4:1)
  • Use Navy text on Gold/Coral button fills
  • Use Teal only for large text or UI accents on white
  • Test every new pairing before shipping

Don't

  • Use Gold or Coral as small text on white
  • Use white text on Teal for small body copy
  • Place light text on light tint backgrounds
  • Rely on color alone to convey meaning — always pair with text or icons
  • Skip contrast checks for "it looks fine to me"

Pairing Rationale

Outfit (geometric sans-serif) is bold and expressive — its rounded terminals and confident weight range signal energy and approachability. Nunito adds warmth in body copy — its rounded humanist forms feel inviting without being childlike.

Together they lean into the "confident, forward-looking" tone the brief calls for.

Rules

Do

  • Use Outfit for headlines, Nunito for body — never reverse
  • Use Nunito for eyebrows and labels in uppercase
  • Keep Outfit at weight 700–800, Nunito at 400–600
  • Maintain clear size hierarchy: headings should be noticeably larger than body
  • Use the same line-height throughout a block of body copy
  • Never combine both fonts in the same sentence

Don't

  • Use Nunito for headlines or hero text
  • Use Outfit for long paragraphs of body copy
  • Set body font larger than or equal to the display font nearby
  • Mix weights randomly — stick to the defined scale
  • Use Outfit at light weights (below 600) — it loses its character
  • Center-align body paragraphs — always left-align

Photography Direction

Photography choices are strategic, not decorative. Every image should pass the demographic and emotional gut-checks.

Subject Guidelines

Do Don't
Real children in real contexts — therapy, focus, play, discovery, collaboration Generic stock: smiling families in white rooms, posed "happy patient" shots
Include older children and teens (8–15 range should feel represented) Only young children or toddlers — 75% of clients are age 8+
Boys and male-presenting subjects featured prominently (70% male client base) Imagery that skews predominantly feminine
Candid and slightly imperfect — real moments of engagement Polished and posed stock-photo aesthetics
Images of moms, families, and clinicians alongside children in below-the-fold sections Abstract illustration alone — real photos create emotional resonance

Emotional Register

Photography should show kids engaged — curious, focused, active, collaborative. Not smiling at nothing. The goal is for a parent to look at a photo and think: "That looks like my kid at their best."

Signature Technique: Art on Photography

The core visual idea: layering illustration or graphic art on top of real photography.

The Photograph

Signals that CSD's program operates in the real world, produces real outcomes, and fits into real family life.

The Art Layer

Lifts the image into possibility, imagination, and aspiration. Together they say: "This is real, and it can be more."

Constraints

Principle Detail
Integration The illustration layer should feel like it belongs with the photograph, not dropped on top. Integration matters more than technique.
Meaning Art should carry meaning — navigational, growth-oriented, or forward-looking imagery. Abstract decoration for its own sake does not belong.
Maturity Must read as mature and considered. Think editorial or studio art direction, not children's app.
Gut-check Would this feel at home on a thoughtful consumer brand aimed at adults who happen to have children? If yes, it's right.

Open Techniques

Pencil sketch, clean vector illustration, fluid painted strokes, or other treatments are all on the table. The style should be harmonious with the graphics used in CSD's virtual and VR sessions.

What to Avoid

Guardrails to keep the brand on track. When in doubt, check against these.

Generic healthcare aesthetic

Nothing that looks like a generic therapy provider site. No blue-and-white medical palette.

Childish or cartoonish elements

No clip art, abstract doodles, floating shapes, or disconnected scribbles. No babyish aesthetic.

Overly literal metaphors

No literal lightbulbs for ideas. Metaphors should be nuanced and brand-specific.

Dated typography

Nothing that reads as "educational software from 2010."

Overcrowded hero sections

Lead with one clear message. The site should feel like an exhale, not an assault.

Passive, vague language

"Empowering families" without saying how. Be specific about what CSD does differently.

Voice & Tone

CSD's brand voice is warm and friendly but professional—hopeful but honest, conversational but credible.

Core Principles

Science Meets Heart

Balance clinical credibility with genuine warmth. Lead with outcomes, follow with empathy.

Partnership Over Prescription

Never prescriptive. Always collaborative. Families are experts on their child; CSD are experts in ABA.

Celebratory but Humble

Spotlight wins without bragging. Acknowledge difficulty with honesty and hope.

Access as a Right

Language is inclusive, multilingual-aware, and never implies care is a luxury or privilege.

Words to Use vs. Avoid

Category Use Avoid
Emotional Partnership, journey, breakthrough, adventure, possibility, joy, together, hope Compliance, productivity, metrics-first language in family-facing copy

Family reference Family, caregiver, your child, parent, loved one Patient, case, client (in family-facing contexts)
